Youngwoo Ji

Abstract—This article presents a methodology to design a
circuit to compensate for process skew by exploiting an inherent
dimension-dependent effect of process skew on change in the
threshold voltage. We design a voltage reference circuit with
a hybrid architecture of bandgap reference (BGR) and CMOS
reference, which generates a nominal voltage level of (bandgap
- threshold). By compensating the process skew of the threshold
term with the proposed dimension-induced effect as well as
the temperature dependence, the circuit achieves the simulta-
neous beneﬁts of BGR and CMOS references. For veriﬁcation,
the circuit was fabricated in three wafers of a 0.18-µm CMOS
including extreme slow and fast corners. With an active area
of 0.0045 mm2, it consumes 192 pW at room temperature. Mea-
surement from 45 chips (15 chips per wafer) shows untrimmed
process/voltage/temperature variations of 0.53%, 0.020%/V, and
33 ppm/◦C, respectively.
Index Terms—Bandgap, process compensation, temperature
compensation, ultra-low power (ULP), voltage reference.
I. INTRODUCTION
I
MPLANTABLE and attachable electronics often require a
nanowatt (nW)-consuming smart system-on-a-chip (SoC)
[1]–[7]. Processing with a duty cycling has been actively
adopted to effectively manage energy consumption. Since the
SoC mostly stays in the sleep mode, the power dissipation
in this mode becomes an important consideration. A voltage
reference circuit is an essential building block, which is often
required to be turned on even during sleep mode to ensure
fast start-up and, therefore, contributes signiﬁcantly to overall
energy consumption.
Voltage reference circuits can be categorized into two types:
bandgap reference (BGR) and CMOS reference. In BGR
circuits, an inherent voltage of the silicon bandgap (VBG) is
associated with the p-n junction. The p-n junction, if conducted
with a current bias, provides a complementary-to-absolute-
temperature (CTAT) voltage that has a large temperature

meters to adjust the temperature coefﬁcient of a proportional-
to-absolute-temperature (PTAT) voltage [8], the reference
generates a voltage level of VBG. For low-power imple-
mentation of BGR, various PTAT generation schemes have
been proposed with differential ampliﬁer [9], capacitor net-
work [10], [11], and use of leakage current for bias [12], [13].
However, complicated circuits with multiple branches require
a large area and preclude further reduction of power usage
into the sub-nW regime.
In CMOS reference circuits, the reference is determined
by the threshold voltage of the MOS transistor (Vth). Vth
typically holds a CTAT characteristic with a smaller temper-
ature dependence than that of BJT. Vita and Iannaccone [14]
and Ueno et al. [15] proposed current-bias circuits that had
a square-root temperature dependence to generate a PTAT.
Flowing this current into a MOS diode leads to a reference
voltage that is close to Vth. To further reduce the power con-
sumption, Magnelli et al. [16] and Seok et al. [17] ﬂow the
leakage current with an exponential temperature dependence
into a MOS diode. In this approach, the level of the reference
voltage is represented as the difference between the threshold
voltages (Vth) of two transistors. Since this difference is too
small to be directly used, it should be ampliﬁed with an up-
scaling [18], which eventually results in an ampliﬁcation of
the standard deviation as well (Table I).
Compared with BGR approaches, CMOS references have
achieved lower power consumption with a smaller area
(Fig. 1). However, the inclusion of the Vth term in the output
results in an inevitable effect of the process variations [19].
The process sensitivity can be reduced by constructing a circuit
that uses only one type of transistor, e.g., only PMOS [20].
Geometry dependence of Vth has also been considered for the
generation of the voltage reference [21]. However, a small
Vth requires a large ampliﬁcation factor to up-scale the out-
put. Process compensation schemes have also been proposed
for current bias [22], [23]. However, they assume a stable
supply voltage which is not ensured for the voltage reference.
This article presents a methodology to compensate for the
process variation in Vth [24]. We propose a voltage reference
circuit with a hybrid architecture of BGR and CMOS ref-
erence; it generates a nominal voltage level of (VBG −Vth).
By compensating for the process skew of the Vth term with the
dimension-induced side effect, the proposed scheme achieves

II. RELATING PROCESS SKEW TO DESIGN PARAMETERS
The dependence of Vth on transistor dimension is affected
by the device-isolation process. There are two representa-
tive schemes: local oxidation of silicon (LOCOS) isolation
and shallow-trench isolation (STI). While LOCOS isolation
exhibits a gradually grown oxidation, STI drastically deﬁnes
device boundaries. Fig. 2 illustrates a cross-sectional view
of a MOS transistor along the width direction for the case
of STI, which is generally taken in the recent CMOS tech-
nologies [25]. A gate voltage generates an electric ﬁeld that
depletes the channel. The fringing ﬁeld on both edges of the
channel is an extra ﬁeld and helps to deplete the channel. As a
result, the fringing ﬁeld effectively reduces Vth. As the channel
width decreases, the relative contribution of the fringing ﬁeld
increases, so the reduction that it causes in Vth also increases.
As substrate doping concentration increases, the process
corner becomes slow-skewed, so the required electric ﬁeld
increases and Vth itself increases. However, the relative portion
of the fringing ﬁeld becomes weaker due to the thinner deple-
tion region [Fig. 2(a)]. In contrast, for a fast-skewed process
corner, the electric ﬁeld to turn on the channel decreases and
Vth itself decreases. However, the relative portion of the fring-
ing ﬁeld becomes stronger due to the thicker depletion region
[Fig. 2(c)]. Since the fringing effect becomes more noticeable
as the channel width decreases [Fig. 3(a)], the difference in
the Vth between slow and fast corners (Vth,SS −Vth,FF) is a
function of the width, where the subscripts SS and FF mean
the slow and the fast corners. The difference increases as
the channel width decreases while it becomes almost constant
when the width is sufﬁciently large [Fig. 3(b)]. This indicates
the process skew affects more signiﬁcantly for the smaller
devices. Vth itself decreases as the process skew goes from
slow to fast corners, but the decrease is increasingly signiﬁcant
as the channel width decreases [Fig. 3(c)].
This relationship suggests a method to include the amount
of process skew as a circuit design parameter. As an example,
given a differential pair that is composed of two input transis-
tors, load resistors, and a current source [Fig. 4(a)], the offset
voltage is

The line regulation and power supply rejection ratio (PSRR)
are the indicators of dc and ac supply sensitivities. To suppress
the supply sensitivity, native NMOS transistors (MN1) are

regulation [30]. In the proposed scheme, the native NMOS
transistors (MN1) and the leakage PMOS transistor (M1)
perform a double regulation, which dramatically ampliﬁes the
effect of the line regulation without affecting the amount of
leakage bias current. M1 sees a low resistance of the common
gate topology formed by MN1. Therefore, the source node of
M1 is effectively isolated from the supply.

IV. MEASUREMENT RESULTS
The proposed circuit was fabricated with a standard
0.18-μm CMOS technology on three different wafers of
process corners (SS, TT, and FF). Fig. 9 shows the chip
micrograph. This article is based on the assumption that the
effect of process variations has well-deﬁned statistics once the
technology of interest is given. The statistics of the process
variations should be included in the SPICE model parameters.
Though the SPICE model has extracted sets of numerical
coefﬁcients and skew parameters that can represent the temper-
ature and process variations, commercially available models
now do not guarantee to precisely cover the extreme range of
pA-leakage level. Therefore, we implemented programmabil-
ity in transistor size for M1 and M2 (Fig. 7) in the chip. While
the widths of unit transistors for M1 and M2 (W1 and W2) were
ﬁxed, the multiplier numbers (N1 and N2) were designed to
be a variable, which can be controlled with a binary-weighted
4 bits. To ﬁnd the optimal codes for sizing, we tested nine
chips of a typical wafer, then we applied these codes to all
the chips; it is deﬁned as “untrimmed” and is ﬁxed throughout

V. CONCLUSION
This article proposes a process/temperature-compensated
voltage reference circuit with a hybrid architecture of BGR
and CMOS references, which generates a nominal voltage
level of (VBG −Vth). Vth is affected by so many technology-
induced factors, but the major effect is originated from a
consequence of a change in doping concentration. However,
once the technology of interest for circuit design is given,
the variations by the skew would have an average with a
limited range of variation. The statistics are even clearly
deﬁned in matured process for mass production. The proposed
design methodology matches the amount of variation range
with the dimension dependence of the Vth variation change.
